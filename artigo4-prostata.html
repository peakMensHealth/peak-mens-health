PSA Test Explained: What Your Numbers Really Mean and What to Do About Them

What PSA Actually Is

PSA stands for Prostate-Specific Antigen ‚Äî a protein produced almost exclusively by prostate cells. Its biological function is to liquefy semen after ejaculation. Under normal circumstances, very little PSA enters the bloodstream. When prostate cells are disrupted ‚Äî by inflammation, infection, benign enlargement, or cancer ‚Äî the barriers keeping PSA inside the prostate break down, and more PSA leaks into circulation.

The critical insight: PSA is prostate-specific, not cancer-specific. Anything that disrupts prostate cells ‚Äî not just cancer ‚Äî raises PSA levels. This is why elevated PSA does not equal prostate cancer, and why interpreting PSA always requires context.

What's "Normal"? PSA Levels by Age

The traditional cutoff of 4.0 ng/mL is increasingly viewed as oversimplified. The prostate grows naturally with age, and more prostate tissue produces more PSA. A PSA of 3.5 ng/mL in a 45-year-old warrants more attention than the same reading in a 70-year-old with a large prostate.

The 7 Things That Raise PSA Besides Cancer

1. Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia (BPH)

The single most common cause of elevated PSA in men over 50. More prostate tissue simply produces more PSA. PSA density ‚Äî PSA divided by prostate volume ‚Äî is more meaningful than raw PSA for men with large prostates.

2. Prostatitis ‚Äî Prostate Inflammation or Infection

Acute bacterial prostatitis can spike PSA to 20, 40, or even 100 ng/mL. PSA should not be measured during an active episode and should be retested 4‚Äì6 weeks after treatment before drawing conclusions about cancer risk.

3. Recent Ejaculation

Ejaculation temporarily raises PSA for 24‚Äì48 hours. Standard guidance recommends abstaining for 48 hours before a PSA test ‚Äî a simple instruction many men are never given, yet one that can prevent unnecessary anxiety from an artificially elevated result.

4. Digital Rectal Examination (DRE)

Physical prostate examination can transiently raise PSA. Either draw blood before the DRE or wait several days after. In borderline cases, this source of artifact can be clinically relevant.

5. Urinary Tract Infection

UTIs cause local inflammation that disrupts the prostate-blood barrier. Always retest PSA after treating any active urinary infection before making clinical decisions based on the elevated reading.

6. Vigorous Exercise ‚Äî Particularly Cycling

Prolonged cycling directly compresses the perineum and prostate, raising PSA by 10‚Äì20%. Avoid vigorous cycling for 48 hours before a PSA test.

7. 5-Alpha Reductase Inhibitor Medications

Finasteride and dutasteride lower PSA by 40‚Äì50%. Men on these medications have artificially suppressed PSA ‚Äî doctors should double the measured value to estimate true PSA. Missing this adjustment leads to missed diagnoses.

PSA Velocity: The Number That Matters More

A single PSA measurement is a snapshot. PSA velocity ‚Äî how fast your PSA is rising over time ‚Äî is the movie. A PSA rise of more than 0.75 ng/mL per year (PSA above 4.0) or 0.35 ng/mL per year (PSA below 4.0) is associated with significantly higher cancer risk and warrants expedited evaluation regardless of the absolute value.

‚ö†Ô∏è PSA Doubling Time ‚Äî The Most Urgent Signal

PSA doubling in less than 3 years is associated with significantly higher risk of aggressive prostate cancer. PSA doubling in less than 12 months is a medical urgency requiring immediate specialist referral. If you have multiple readings over time, ask your doctor to calculate your doubling time.

Free PSA Ratio: Refining the Picture

PSA in the blood exists as protein-bound and free forms. Cancer cells produce more bound PSA and less free PSA, so a lower free PSA percentage suggests higher cancer risk. For men with total PSA between 4‚Äì10 ng/mL:

  • Free PSA above 25%: ~8% cancer probability ‚Äî lower risk
  • Free PSA 15‚Äì25%: ~16‚Äì24% cancer probability ‚Äî intermediate
  • Free PSA below 10%: ~49‚Äì56% cancer probability ‚Äî higher risk

Lifestyle Factors That Lower PSA

Weight loss: A 10% reduction in body weight in overweight men consistently produces measurable PSA reductions through reduced inflammation and estrogen levels.

Anti-inflammatory diet: The Mediterranean dietary pattern ‚Äî high in cooked tomatoes, cruciferous vegetables, omega-3s, and olive oil ‚Äî has demonstrated PSA-lowering effects in clinical studies. Lycopene from cooked tomatoes has the most direct evidence.

Exercise: Both aerobic and resistance training reduce systemic inflammation and improve insulin sensitivity, lowering PSA for a given prostate volume.

Stress reduction: Chronic cortisol elevation promotes inflammation relevant to PSA trajectory. Adequate sleep, mindfulness, and stress management produce measurable anti-inflammatory effects.

Frequently Asked Questions

My doctor says my PSA is "normal" but I'm still worried. What should I ask?
Ask for your actual number and compare it to the age-adjusted ranges above. Ask whether your PSA has been tested before and what the trend is. Ask about your free PSA ratio if your total PSA is above 2.5. A "normal" label without context can be misleading.
When should I start getting PSA tested?
Age 50 for average-risk men; age 45 with a first-degree relative diagnosed before age 65; age 40 for men with multiple affected relatives or men of African descent. Establishing a baseline in your 40s is valuable for velocity tracking over time.
Is a biopsy always necessary with elevated PSA?
No. Current guidelines support an MRI-first approach. Men with low-suspicion MRI findings and borderline PSA can often be safely monitored without immediate biopsy. The decision depends on PSA level, velocity, free PSA ratio, MRI findings, age, and overall health.
My PSA went up between two tests. Should I panic?
Not immediately. Rule out benign causes first: recent ejaculation, vigorous exercise, any infection. Retest in 4‚Äì6 weeks under controlled conditions. If the elevated reading persists or shows a consistent upward trend, then pursue further evaluation ‚Äî but a single elevated reading requires confirmation before action.
